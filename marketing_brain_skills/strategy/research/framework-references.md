## 1. Reforge growth strategy framework

**What it is:**

Reforge is a practitioner-developed growth strategy curriculum built by Brian Balfour (former VP Growth at HubSpot), Andrew Chen (a16z), Casey Winters (Pinterest, Eventbrite), and others. The core Reforge product growth model identifies three growth loops that compound over time: (a) a paid acquisition loop (spend money to acquire users who generate revenue that funds more spend), (b) a viral or word-of-mouth loop (users refer other users), and (c) a content or SEO loop (content assets accumulate and generate organic traffic that compounds). [Source: Reforge.com, Growth Series curriculum, 2024; Brian Balfour, "Why You Should Find Product-Market Fit Before Growth," Reforge blog, 2024.]

The Reforge framework's insight for marketing-driven businesses is that content loops compound differently than paid loops. A paid loop stops the moment spend stops. A content loop accumulates: an article posted in 2023 still generates traffic in 2026. The strategic implication is that investing early in the content loop creates a compounding asset, while investing only in paid acquisition creates a treadmill.

**Application to Ryan Realty:**

Ryan Realty is a content-loop business at its core, not a paid-loop business. Each market report video, each neighborhood guide, each SEO blog post is an asset that continues to generate views, leads, and brand recognition after publication. The paid Meta ad loop (Facebook seller growth pipeline) is the short-term lead engine, but it is expensive and stops the moment spend pauses.

The Q3 2026 strategy should prioritize building the content loop to the point where it generates consistent inbound leads without requiring paid spend. The target metric for this transition is: "content-attributable seller leads as a percentage of total seller leads rising from its current baseline to 40%+ by end of Q4 2026."

The Reforge framework also identifies "retention loops" as compounders. For Ryan Realty, the retention loop is the relationship with past clients who refer future clients. The email newsletter (ops-email-send producer) and the GBP review response strategy (ops-reputation producer) both feed this loop.

**Q3 recommendation from this framework:** Prioritize the content loop (market reports, neighborhood guides, SEO blogs) over paid loop expansion in Q3. Keep paid at maintenance budget while the content loop builds. Measure content-attributed leads monthly.

## 2. Jobs-to-be-done (JTBD)

**What it is:**

Jobs-to-be-done theory (Clayton Christensen, Harvard Business School; Bob Moesta, ReWired Group; Alan Klement) posits that customers do not buy products or services. They "hire" products and services to do a job in their life. The job is defined by the progress the customer is trying to make in a specific situation. [Source: Clayton Christensen, Taddy Hall, Karen Dillon, David S. Duncan, "Competing Against Luck," Harper Business, 2016; Bob Moesta and Chris Spiek, "The Demand-Side Sales Handbook," 2020.]

The JTBD framework separates the "job" (functional progress sought) from the "struggle moment" (the event that triggers the hire) and the "outcome" (what success looks like). Understanding all three lets a business build marketing that resonates with the customer's actual internal narrative, not the business's self-narrative.

**Application to Ryan Realty:**

The JTBD lens reveals four distinct jobs Ryan Realty clients hire for:

**Job 1: "Help me sell my Bend property without being physically present."**
Struggle moment: Inherited a Bend property, or moved out of state for work, or retired to another state, and now need to sell without flying back repeatedly.
Outcome: Property sold at a fair price with minimal time investment from the client.
What content resonates: The "how selling from out of state works" explainer. The market data video that tells the out-of-state owner what the market is doing. The agent-accessibility message (texts answered, calls returned same day).

**Job 2: "Help me understand if now is the right time to sell."**
Struggle moment: Homeowner has owned for 5-10+ years, has seen price appreciation, is not sure if values are near a peak or still rising, and does not want to sell too late.
Outcome: A clear, honest picture of where the market is and what it means for their specific situation.
What content resonates: Monthly market reports with specific price trajectory data. The months-of-supply chart. The absorption rate by neighborhood.

**Job 3: "Help me buy in Bend before I get priced out."**
Struggle moment: Remote worker or relocating professional who has been watching Bend prices for 12-24 months, fears prices will continue rising, wants to make a move before it becomes unaffordable.
Outcome: A home in the right neighborhood at a price that makes sense for their situation.
What content resonates: Neighborhood-specific guides that compare price points. First-time buyer process explainers. Cost-of-living in Bend versus origin city.

**Job 4: "Help me buy or sell Bend investment property."**
Struggle moment: Investor who owns STR or rental property in Bend and needs to optimize for yield, or is evaluating a Sunriver or Tetherow purchase for rental income.
Outcome: Investment property acquired or sold at the right price with STR permit status and rental income data clearly presented.
What content resonates: STR market data. Cap rate analysis. Short-term rental rule summaries by neighborhood.

**Q3 recommendation from this framework:** Map every planned content piece to one of these four jobs. Content that does not serve one of the four jobs does not belong in the Q3 calendar. Ensure Job 1 (out-of-state seller) content represents at least 40% of seller-funnel content, as it is the primary north-star lead driver.

## 3. Eugene Schwartz awareness ladder

**What it is:**

Eugene Schwartz (copywriter, author of "Breakthrough Advertising," 1966) identified five stages of buyer awareness that determine how to write copy for any product or service. [Source: Eugene M. Schwartz, "Breakthrough Advertising," Bottom Line Books, 2004 (reprint).] The five stages:

- Stage 1 (Most Aware): The prospect knows your product, knows what it does, just needs the deal.
- Stage 2 (Product-Aware): The prospect knows your product but is not sure it is right for them.
- Stage 3 (Solution-Aware): The prospect knows a solution exists but does not know your specific product.
- Stage 4 (Problem-Aware): The prospect knows they have a problem but does not know there is a solution.
- Stage 5 (Unaware): The prospect has the problem but does not recognize it as a problem.

**Application to Ryan Realty:**

Real estate marketing almost universally targets Stage 1 and Stage 2 (people who are actively shopping for an agent). The competitive differentiation opportunity is in Stage 4 and Stage 5.

Stage 4 example: A Bend homeowner who bought in 2018 at $380,000 does not know their home is now worth $620,000 or that they could use that equity to buy their next home before selling. They have a problem (they could be making a financial move) but do not recognize it. Ryan Realty's market report content, when framed around "what your equity looks like right now," speaks to Stage 4 prospects.

Stage 5 example: A Bend homeowner who is vaguely dissatisfied with their current home (too small, school district changed, neighborhood character shifted) but has not yet framed this as "I should sell." Lifestyle content that opens with "when the house that worked for you in 2019 doesn't work for you anymore" speaks to Stage 5 prospects.

The Facebook seller growth pipeline (CLAUDE.md, docs/FACEBOOK_SELLER_GROWTH_PIPELINE.md) is currently targeting Stage 3 (solution-aware: they know they can sell, they're looking for an agent). The awareness ladder suggests that adding Stage 4 and Stage 5 content to the content mix could expand the top of the funnel before the competition enters.

**Q3 recommendation from this framework:** Develop one Stage 4 content series ("Your Bend equity right now: what 2020 buyers hold") and one Stage 5 content series ("Five signs your house no longer fits"). Run these as a monthly blog series and corresponding social content. These are brand-building investments with longer conversion cycles, but they build the top of the seller funnel.

## 4. Russell Brunson value ladder

**What it is:**

Russell Brunson (founder, ClickFunnels) documented the value ladder concept in "DotCom Secrets" (2015) and "Expert Secrets" (2017). [Source: Russell Brunson, "DotCom Secrets," Morgan James Publishing, 2015.] The value ladder says: start with a free or very low-cost offer that delivers immediate value, then ascend the customer through progressively higher-value and higher-price offers. Each rung on the ladder deepens the relationship and increases trust.

In consumer services, the value ladder is most relevant as a relationship-building model rather than a pure upsell model.

**Application to Ryan Realty:**

Ryan Realty's value ladder has five rungs.

Rung 1 (Free, no commitment): Market reports, neighborhood guides, market data videos. Consumed with no contact required.
Rung 2 (Low commitment, no money): Home valuation tool. 60 seconds of effort, email captured, initial relationship established.
Rung 3 (Medium commitment, no money): Phone call or in-person consultation. 30 minutes of conversation, relationship deepened, seller intent assessed.
Rung 4 (High commitment, listing agreement): The listing is signed. Ryan Realty represents the seller. The brokerage relationship is active.
Rung 5 (Completed transaction, ongoing relationship): Sale closed. Ryan Realty is the "Bend real estate resource" for this client permanently. Referrals flow from here.

The value ladder insight for Q3 is that Rungs 1 and 2 need the most attention. Most Ryan Realty content marketing touches people at Rung 1. The conversion from Rung 1 to Rung 2 (from content consumer to home valuation submitter) is the primary leverage point. Every piece of content should include a clear path to the home valuation tool.

**Q3 recommendation from this framework:** Every market data video, neighborhood guide, and blog post ends with a single CTA pointing to the home valuation tool. Every social caption includes the home valuation URL in the first comment or link-in-bio reference. The Rung 1 to Rung 2 conversion is the single most important funnel metric to improve in Q3.

## 5. McKinsey 7-S framework

**What it is:**

The McKinsey 7-S framework (Tom Peters and Robert Waterman, "In Search of Excellence," 1982; McKinsey & Company internal use from 1980s) identifies seven interdependent elements of organizational effectiveness: Strategy, Structure, Systems, Staff, Skills, Style, and Shared Values. The insight is that changing one element without adjusting the others produces friction. All seven elements must be aligned for an organization to execute effectively. [Source: Tom Peters and Robert Waterman, "In Search of Excellence," Harper & Row, 1982; McKinsey & Company, "The McKinsey 7S Framework," mckinsey.com.]

**Application to Ryan Realty:**

Ryan Realty's seven elements as of Q2 2026:

- **Strategy:** Data-first single-market brokerage building authority through content and an autonomous marketing pipeline.
- **Structure:** Principal broker (Matt) supported by Paul Stevenson and Rebecca Peterson, plus the AI-driven autonomous pipeline.
- **Systems:** Supabase as the data layer, FUB as the CRM, Remotion as the video renderer, ElevenLabs as the voice synthesis layer, the marketing brain as the content orchestration layer.
- **Staff:** Three licensed brokers, one autonomous pipeline (effectively a fourth team member).
- **Skills:** Local market knowledge, content production capability, data analysis via the pipeline, CRM management.
- **Style:** Low-pressure, data-driven, fiduciary-first, honest even when inconvenient.
- **Shared Values:** Real estate is for everyone. Represent the client as a fiduciary. A resolution exists for almost every problem.

The 7-S analysis reveals one potential misalignment: the Systems element (sophisticated autonomous pipeline) is ahead of the Staff element (three brokers plus the pipeline). As the pipeline scales content output, the staff capacity to handle inbound leads and consult with clients must scale in parallel. If the pipeline generates 30 qualified seller leads per month and the three brokers cannot serve all 30, the systems are outrunning the staff.

**Q3 recommendation from this framework:** Add a lead-routing and capacity-management protocol to the strategy. At what lead volume does Ryan Realty need a fourth broker? What is the revenue trigger for that hire? Build the capacity model into the Q3-2026-strategy.md alongside the content plan.

## 6. Porter's Five Forces

**What it is:**

Michael Porter's Five Forces framework (Harvard Business School, "Competitive Strategy," 1980) analyzes competitive intensity in an industry across five dimensions: (1) threat of new entrants, (2) bargaining power of buyers, (3) bargaining power of suppliers, (4) threat of substitute products or services, and (5) rivalry among existing competitors. [Source: Michael E. Porter, "Competitive Strategy," Free Press, 1980.]

**Application to Ryan Realty's Q3 positioning:**

**Threat of new entrants (medium-high):** Oregon's real estate license requirements create a modest barrier to entry for individual agents, but the barrier to brokerage entry is low for Compass, eXp, or Real affiliates who can set up a Bend presence rapidly. The more significant threat is an established Portland brokerage (Hasson, John L. Scott) expanding Bend presence. Counter: local knowledge advantage, established GBP authority, and the content library are local moats that a new entrant cannot replicate quickly.

**Bargaining power of buyers (medium):** In a seller's market (Bend months of supply 3.8 as of April 2026), buyers have limited power. In a balanced or buyer's market, buyers shop more aggressively on commission and service quality. Ryan Realty's data-first approach is a stronger competitive position in a softer market than a pure buyer's market where price becomes the primary differentiator.

**Bargaining power of suppliers (low for most, high for MLS access):** The primary "supplier" is the MLS (COAR/ORMLS). MLS membership is required for listing access and is a regulated cost. Data feeds (Spark API) are a secondary supplier. Both have low bargaining power relative to Ryan Realty.

**Threat of substitutes (high and growing):** Discount and flat-fee brokerage models (Redfin, Opendoor, iBuyer programs) represent substitutes for the full-service brokerage model. Ryan Realty's response is to demonstrate that the full-service model produces better outcomes for sellers through better pricing, more exposure, and faster transactions. The data content strategy directly addresses this by showing market pricing intelligence that a DIY seller would not have.

**Rivalry among existing competitors (high):** Eight named competitors in Bend (Cascade Sotheby's, Hasson, John L. Scott Bend, Compass Bend, EXIT Realty Bend, Coldwell Banker Sun Country, RE/MAX Key Properties, BHHS NW Real Estate) plus the national disruptors. The competitive rivalry is primarily in the $400K-$1.2M SFR segment where most transactions occur.

**Q3 recommendation from this framework:** The Porter analysis confirms that Ryan Realty's strongest moat is local knowledge combined with content-based authority. Investing in the content library in Q3 builds the moat against both new entrants and substitutes. The threat of substitutes (flat-fee models) is best answered by producing content that demonstrates the expertise difference, not by competing on price.

## 7. Ansoff Growth Matrix

**What it is:**

Igor Ansoff's growth matrix (Harvard Business Review, 1957) maps growth strategies across two axes: existing vs. new markets, and existing vs. new products/services. The four quadrants: (1) Market Penetration (existing product, existing market), (2) Market Development (existing product, new market), (3) Product Development (new product, existing market), and (4) Diversification (new product, new market). [Source: Igor Ansoff, "Strategies for Diversification," Harvard Business Review, September-October 1957.]

**Application to Ryan Realty Q3 2026:**

**Market Penetration (existing service, existing Bend market):** This is Ryan Realty's core Q3 strategy. Increase market share in the Bend SFR segment through better content, stronger GBP presence, and more systematic lead follow-up. Risk is low. Return is predictable.

**Market Development (existing service, new geographic markets):** Redmond, Sisters, Prineville, La Pine, and Sunriver are adjacent markets Ryan Realty already serves but does not heavily target with dedicated content. La Pine (§1.16 of bend-market-bible.md) and Redmond both represent legitimate market development opportunities with minimal incremental cost.

**Product Development (new service, existing Bend market):** Three potential new services: (a) investor advisory (STR analysis, cap rate modeling), (b) relocation concierge (employer introductions, neighborhood matching, moving logistics coordination), (c) property management light (connecting past clients with vetted property managers for STRs or LTRs). All three serve the existing Bend client base with a service extension.

**Diversification (new service, new market):** Not recommended for Q3. Spread too thin.

**Q3 recommendation from this framework:** Focus Q3 resources on Market Penetration (70%) and targeted Market Development into Redmond/La Pine/Sunriver (20%). Explore one Product Development concept (investor advisory content series) as a 10% investment. No Diversification in Q3.
