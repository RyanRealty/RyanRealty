## 2. Tourism and STR market dynamics

**STR market scale:**
Bend and Sunriver together represent one of the highest STR concentration areas in Oregon. As of 2025, approximately 2,800-3,200 active short-term rental units in the greater Bend-Sunriver area. [Source: Oregon Department of Revenue transient lodging tax data, 2024; City of Bend Planning Division STR permit count, 2025. [Unverified: DOR data is available annually; specific 2025 count requires agency request.]]

**City of Bend STR permit reality:**
The City of Bend permits STRs only in specific zones. As of 2025, STRs are not permitted in most residential zones within city limits unless the owner occupies the property as their primary residence (Hosted STRs). Non-hosted STRs (absent-owner) are permitted only in certain commercial or mixed-use zones or within existing resort overlay areas. [Source: City of Bend STR regulations, bendoregon.gov/services/development-permitting/short-term-rentals, 2025.]

This is a significant compliance risk for content producers. Any listing that includes STR income language must be verified against the actual permit status of the specific property before publication. Bend's STR regulations are stricter than many sellers and buyers assume.

**Sunriver STR reality:**
Sunriver (under SROA jurisdiction, not City of Bend) has a more permissive STR framework within the resort overlay. STRs are common throughout much of the Sunriver property inventory. SROA rules govern rental management and noise/nuisance standards, but the basic right to rent short-term is established for most Sunriver residential parcels. [Source: Sunriver Owners Association, sroa.org, 2025.]

**Tetherow STR status:**
Tetherow's STR permit status varies by sub-community within the resort. Some areas permit non-hosted STRs; others restrict to hosted or prohibit entirely. The Tetherow master developer has amended STR rules multiple times since 2020. Listing producers must verify the specific sub-community's current permit status directly with Tetherow's HOA or the City of Bend Planning Division before including any STR income language. [Source: Tetherow Golf Resort community office, 2025. [Unverified: requires direct verification with Tetherow HOA for current rules.]]

**Tourism volume:**
Bend attracted approximately 3.2 million visitors in 2024, generating approximately $560 million in visitor spending. [Source: Visit Bend, 2024 Visitor Economy Impact Report, visitbend.com. [Unverified: Visit Bend publishes annual reports; this figure is directionally consistent with their 2022-2023 published figures.]] Tourism is the foundation of the STR market demand. Tourism growth supports STR occupancy rates.

**Mt. Bachelor as a traffic driver:**
Mt. Bachelor averages approximately 40 inches of annual snowfall base-level and 400+ inches at summit elevation. It is one of the top 10 US ski resorts by skier visits in most years. [Source: NSAA (National Ski Areas Association), skier visit data, 2024-2025 season.] The Mt. Bachelor ski season (typically December-April) creates a winter tourist surge that drives STR occupancy in Tetherow, Awbrey Butte, and NW Bend. Sellers in these areas benefit from listing in spring after the winter rental season demonstrates strong STR income to potential buyers.

## 3. Wildfire insurance trends

**The insurance crisis context:**
California's wildfire insurance crisis has begun to affect Oregon, including Central Oregon. As of 2025, several major insurers (State Farm, Allstate, Farmers) have restricted new policy issuance in high-risk areas of Oregon, including parts of Deschutes County. This is documented in Oregon Insurance Division (OID) non-renewal and cancellation data. [Source: Oregon Insurance Division, Annual Report 2024; OPB (Oregon Public Broadcasting), coverage of wildfire insurance restrictions, 2024-2025.]

**Specific risk to Bend real estate transactions:**
Insurance availability and affordability is now a material factor in real estate transactions in wildland-urban interface (WUI) areas in Deschutes County. Properties within or adjacent to high-risk fire zones may face:
- Non-renewal of existing policies at closing
- Forced placement in the Oregon FAIR Plan (last-resort insurance, higher cost, limited coverage)
- Lender requirements for additional fire coverage that increases monthly carrying cost
- Buyer walkouts when insurance costs make the property unaffordable

The Oregon FAIR Plan is the state's insurer of last resort. Premiums on FAIR Plan policies can be 2-5× standard market rates. [Source: Oregon Insurance Division, FAIR Plan information, ins.oregon.gov. [Unverified: specific premium range requires direct FAIR Plan inquiry.]]

**Ryan Realty content implication:**
Every listing in a WUI area must include insurance disclosure language. Producers must not represent a property as "easy to insure" without verification. The listing-description producer and the listing-tour-video producer should both include a "buyers should verify insurance availability" statement for WUI-adjacent properties.

The wildfire insurance crisis is also a content opportunity. A "how wildfire insurance works in Bend" blog post targets a high-intent search query from buyers considering Bend properties. This is an educational content piece that builds trust while serving a genuine buyer information need.

**High-risk neighborhoods (approximate, producers must verify against current ODF/OFM risk maps):**
Tumalo, Vandevert Ranch, Three Rivers, Tetherow's western boundary, parts of NW Bend adjacent to Shevlin Park, and rural properties in the Deschutes National Forest interface are highest-risk. [Source: Oregon Department of Forestry (ODF) Community Protection Zone maps; Oregon State Fire Marshal (OFM) Community Risk Summary maps, 2024.]

## 4. Water-rights pressure

**Context:**
Central Oregon is in a high desert climate. Annual precipitation in Bend is approximately 11.8 inches. Water supply is governed by Oregon water law, which operates on prior appropriation ("first in time, first in right"). Irrigation water for agricultural properties in Deschutes County comes primarily from three irrigation districts: Tumalo Irrigation District (TID), Swalley Irrigation District, and Central Oregon Irrigation District (COID). [Source: Oregon Water Resources Department (OWRD), water rights database, oregon.gov/owrd; Deschutes River Alliance, 2025.]

**The Deschutes River compact:**
Oregon, under OWRD oversight, has managed the Deschutes River basin under a multi-party water agreement since 2002. Groundwater and surface water rights are increasingly constrained as basin demand has grown faster than natural recharge. New groundwater rights in the Deschutes basin are effectively not being issued for residential or agricultural purposes. This has significant implications for rural properties. [Source: Oregon Water Resources Department, Deschutes Basin Study, 2023.]

**What this means for Tumalo and rural listings:**
A Tumalo property listed with "irrigated acreage" is a materially different property than one without irrigation rights. The presence of a TID or Swalley water right is a significant value factor. Producers who write copy or produce video for a Tumalo or rural Deschutes County listing must verify and name the irrigation district, the water right type (primary or secondary), the annual allocation in acre-feet, and any pending curtailment orders. This is a data accuracy requirement per CLAUDE.md §0, not just a nice-to-have. [Source: bend-market-bible.md §1.10; CLAUDE.md §0.]

**Groundwater well implications:**
For properties on private wells (common in Tumalo, Three Rivers, La Pine), Oregon law requires well disclosure. OWRD's Water Resources Information System (WRIS) provides well log records. Producers must not claim "abundant water supply" for any property on a private well without referencing the well log and any known quality test data. [Source: OWRD, WRIS database; bend-market-bible.md §4.]

**Future risk:**
The Oregon Water Resources Department has increased enforcement of unapproved water withdrawals in the Deschutes basin. Properties that have historically used water beyond their permitted right face potential curtailment. This is an emerging disclosure risk for rural properties. [Source: OWRD enforcement announcements, 2024-2025.]

## 5. ADU policy: strategy-specific depth

**The June 2025 rule change:**
The City of Bend City Council approved allowing up to two ADUs on a single residential lot in June 2025. Prior to this change, most residential lots allowed only one ADU. This change materially increases the income potential and redevelopment optionality of standard residential lots throughout Bend. [Source: City of Bend ADU Resource Hub, bendoregon.gov; Bend City Council meeting minutes, June 2025.]

**Content opportunity:**
The ADU policy change is one of the most significant changes to Bend residential zoning in a decade. It creates a content opportunity that is directly relevant to multiple buyer and seller personas:
- Seller persona: "Your lot could support two ADUs under the new rules. Here is what that means for your property value."
- Buyer persona: "New ADU rules let you build income on any Bend residential lot."
- Investor persona: "How to underwrite a Bend lot purchase with ADU development potential."

A specific blog series targeting these three angles could rank for "Bend ADU rules 2025," "Bend ADU income," and "Bend accessory dwelling unit" queries. These are emerging queries that should have limited competition as of Q3 2026.

**ADU financing:**
Oregon Housing and Community Services (OHCS) runs an ADU loan program for qualifying homeowners. The program provides low-interest loans for ADU construction. [Source: OHCS, ADU Loan Program, oregon.gov/ohcs. [Unverified: program availability and terms change; requires OHCS confirmation for current status.]] Referencing this program in ADU content adds practical value for readers.

## 6. Bend planning roadmap

**Bend Urban Growth Boundary (UGB) expansion:**
Bend completed its most recent Urban Growth Boundary expansion in 2016, adding approximately 2,170 acres to the city's developable land base. The next UGB expansion process is expected to begin in the 2027-2030 timeframe, depending on population growth and DLCD (Oregon Department of Land Conservation and Development) review. [Source: City of Bend Planning Division, Bend 2030 Growth Management Plan; Oregon DLCD, Goal 14 Urban Growth Management, 2025.]

**What this means for Q3 content:** The outer edges of Bend's current city limits (NE Bend, SE Bend expansion areas) will be the likely focus of the next UGB expansion. Properties in these transition zones may benefit from long-term development upside. This is a nuanced point that belongs in a blog post, not a social media caption, but it is a real market intelligence point for buyers considering the outer rings.

**Transportation planning:**
The US 97 Bend Parkway and Bend Parkway Connectivity Project is an ongoing ODOT initiative to improve freight and commuter flow through Bend. Phase 1 work continues through 2025-2026. [Source: ODOT Bend Parkway project page, oregon.gov/odot/projects.] For sellers on the west side, reduced traffic noise is a coming amenity as the parkway improves connectivity and reduces surface-street through-traffic. For buyers, understanding the construction timeline is relevant to quality-of-life during the construction phase.

**OSU-Cascades campus growth:**
As noted in §1, OSU-Cascades is targeting 2,200 students by fall 2030. The campus is developing a full residential and academic campus on SW Century Drive. Student housing, faculty housing, and campus-adjacent commercial development are planned for the 2024-2030 period. This will create sustained demand for housing in NW Crossing and NW Bend adjacent to the campus. [Source: OSU-Cascades Campus Master Plan, oregonstate.edu/cascades.]

**High Desert Housing Trust:**
Bend has an increasingly active affordable housing nonprofit sector. High Desert Housing Partnership and Bend Housing Authority manage workforce housing programs. The City's SDC (Systems Development Charge) waiver program for ADU construction is designed to reduce barriers for workforce housing creation. [Source: City of Bend, SDC Waiver for ADUs, bendoregon.gov. [Unverified: current program terms require confirmation.]]

**Content implication:** The planning roadmap supports content around "the future of NW Bend" and "what Bend's growth means for your property value." These are mid-funnel content pieces that attract homeowners who are thinking about selling in the next 2-5 years, not necessarily right now. They build the relationship that converts to a listing in a future quarter.
